Jonathan Sun

Die Rolling

The package "dieroller" is a minimal implementation for simulating rolling a die a given number of times, and to visualize the relative frequencies in such a series of rolls.

Creating a die

The first step is to create a "die" object with the function die():

mydie <- die()
mydie
#> object "die"
#> 
#>   side      prob
#> 1    1 0.1666667
#> 2    2 0.1666667
#> 3    3 0.1666667
#> 4    4 0.1666667
#> 5    5 0.1666667
#> 6    6 0.1666667

By default, die() creates a fair die with standard sides 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and with probability values for each side of 1/6.

You can change the defatuls with arguments sides and prob:

loaded <- die(sides = c('a', 'b', 'c', 'd', 'e', 'f'), prob = c(0.075, 0.1, 0.125, 0.15, 0.20, 0.35))
loaded
#> object "die"
#> 
#>   side  prob
#> 1    a 0.075
#> 2    b 0.100
#> 3    c 0.125
#> 4    d 0.150
#> 5    e 0.200
#> 6    f 0.350

Rolling a die

Once you have defined a "die" object, you can use the function roll() to roll a die a given number of times. The output will be an object of class "roll", which will contain the vector of rolls:

roll100 <- roll(mydie, times = 100)
roll100
#> object "roll"
#> 
#> $rolls
#> 5 5 4 6 5 1 5 1 5 4 1 6 4 2 5 4 1 6 1 4 1 2 3 6 1 5 5 2 1 2 2 1 5 4 1 2 3 6 3 3 3 6 2 6 3 4 5 2 2 1 5 6 1 6 1 2 1 1 6 4 2 4 3 1 4 3 6 1 4 6 2 3 1 6 6 4 5 6 1 4 2 5 6 2 3 5 6 1 4 5 6 1 1 2 5 4 1 6 2 6

The summary() function gives you a nice table of frequencies:

summary(roll100)
#> summary "roll"
#> 
#>   side count prop
#> 1    1    23 0.23
#> 2    2    16 0.16
#> 3    3    10 0.10
#> 4    4    15 0.15
#> 5    5    16 0.16
#> 6    6    20 0.20

Plot of relative frequencies

You can use the function plot() to create a graohic with the relative frequencies of a series of die rolls:

plot(roll100)
